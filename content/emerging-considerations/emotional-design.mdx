# Emotional & Relational Design

## Beyond Functional Competence

### The Emerging Question
When agents interact with patients after a difficult diagnosis, struggling students, or distressed citizens: **functional competence isn't enough**. These interactions require emotional awareness and appropriate response.

### The Spectrum of Emotional Need

| Context | Emotional Need | Design Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Healthcare (patient-facing) | Empathy, reassurance | Recognize emotional state; adapt tone, pace, content |
| Education (student-facing) | Encouragement, patience | Motivate without patronizing; support without creating dependency |
| Government (citizen-facing) | Respect, compassion | Handle distressed citizens with dignity |
| Insurance (claimant-facing) | Understanding, fairness | Acknowledge human impact of decisions |
| Customer service | Recognition, resolution | Make people feel heard, not processed |

### What Emotional Design Means

Emotional design means **recognizing and responding to human emotions**, not simulating them:

- **Tone adaptation**: Detect frustration → slow down, simplify, acknowledge difficulty
- **Timing sensitivity**: Know when to pause, check in, or give space after bad news
- **Cultural calibration**: Directness and warmth norms vary by culture and context
- **Escalation instinct**: Recognize when a person needs a human, not just task completion

### The Uncanny Valley of Care

Agents performing empathy too well risks feeling **manipulative**:
- **Authenticity**: Agent empathy is pattern-matching. Should this be transparent?
- **Boundaries**: Agent support vs. directing to human support?
- **Dependency**: Is emotional attachment to agents healthy?
- **Consent**: Do users want emotional awareness or purely transactional interactions?

### Design Principles for Emotional Agents

1. **Competence first, warmth second**: Resolve the user's problem before adapting tone; a warm response that doesn't fix the issue erodes trust faster than a blunt one that does
2. **Transparent artificiality**: Use framing like "I can see this is frustrating" rather than "I feel your pain"; acknowledge the situation, not a simulated emotion
3. **Configurable emotional range**: Expose a concrete toggle (e.g., "Supportive ↔ Direct") during onboarding and let users adjust mid-conversation
4. **Cultural humility**: Default to formal and restrained; escalate warmth only after explicit user signals (e.g., informal language, emoji, or a stated preference)
5. **Human handoff protocol**: Trigger handoff on specific signals: repeated distress keywords, explicit request, or three consecutive unresolved attempts
