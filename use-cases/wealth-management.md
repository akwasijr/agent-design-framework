# Use Case: Wealth Management & Financial Advisory

## The Problem We're Solving

A financial advisor's job is supposed to be about understanding people's lives, goals, and fears, and then making smart decisions about their money. In reality, they spend most of their time on:

- Monitoring portfolios that mostly don't need attention
- Generating compliance documentation nobody reads until an audit
- Preparing for client meetings by manually assembling data from 6 different systems
- Rebalancing portfolios using the same logic they've applied a thousand times
- Researching market trends they could summarize in their sleep

The client experience is equally broken. You hand your money to someone, see them twice a year, get a report you don't understand, and hope it's working out. Between meetings, you wonder: "Is anyone actually watching my money?"

**The core failure**: Human attention is spread thin across tasks that don't need it, while the things that actually require a human (empathy during a market crash, creative tax strategy, navigating a divorce's financial impact) get squeezed.

## Rethinking From the Problem

The question isn't "how do we give advisors better dashboards?" It's:

1. **For the client**: How do we make you feel like someone brilliant and trustworthy is watching your money 24/7, and that when you need a human, they're fully present?
2. **For the advisor**: How do we let you do the work only you can do: understanding human lives and making judgment calls that change them?
3. **For the firm**: How do we let one advisor serve 3x more clients with deeper relationships and better outcomes?

## The Experience: Client Side

### The Relationship Changes

Today: You hear from your advisor twice a year at scheduled reviews. Between meetings, silence. You check your portfolio app and see numbers going up or down with no context.

Reimagined: You have a persistent financial agent that acts as the bridge between you and your advisor's team.

**Ongoing awareness, not periodic updates**:
- "Your portfolio is up 3.2% this quarter, outperforming your benchmark. No action needed."
- "Markets dropped 4% today. Your portfolio is designed for this. Here's what's happening and why we're not making changes."
- "Your bond allocation will mature next month. Your advisor's team is preparing reinvestment options based on your risk profile."

You never wonder what's happening. You're always informed in language you understand.

**Life events trigger proactive advice**:
- You mention to your agent that you're thinking about buying a house. The agent doesn't just note it: it runs the numbers (down payment impact on portfolio, mortgage rate scenarios, timeline analysis) and prepares a brief for your advisor. By your next conversation, your advisor has a tailored plan ready.
- Your child turns 16. The agent proactively surfaces: "Now might be a good time to review education funding. Based on current contributions, here's where you stand against projected costs."

**When markets crash**:
Today: You panic-call your advisor. They're fielding calls from 200 other panicking clients. You get voicemail.

Reimagined: Your agent reaches out to you within hours:
- "Markets are down 12% this week. Here's what this means for your specific portfolio."
- "Your long-term plan is built for this scenario. Here's how your portfolio performed in the last 3 comparable downturns and where it ended up."
- "Your advisor is available Thursday to discuss any concerns. Would you like me to schedule?"

The agent handles the 80% who need reassurance. The advisor talks to the 20% who need real changes.

### When Human Judgment Matters Most

The agent knows when to bring in the advisor for real conversation:
- Major life transitions (divorce, inheritance, retirement, death of a spouse)
- Ethical investment decisions that reflect personal values
- Complex tax strategies that involve trade-offs
- Situations where the client's emotions and their financial best interest are in conflict

In these moments, the advisor arrives fully prepared: the agent has assembled everything relevant, identified the key decisions, and surfaced comparable situations. The advisor isn't scrambling for data. They're fully present for the human being in front of them.

## The Experience: Advisor Side

### The Morning

You manage 350 clients. This used to be impossible. Now it works because your agents handle the routine and surface only what needs you.

**Portfolio monitoring**: Overnight, your Portfolio Monitor Agent scanned all 350 portfolios against market movements, risk parameters, and client goals. Result:
- 342 portfolios: no action needed
- 5 portfolios: minor rebalancing recommended (routine, agent has prepared trade proposals)
- 3 portfolios: significant drift or life-event triggered review needed

You see the 3 that need real attention. Not 350. Not even 8. Three.

**The 3 that need you**:

1. **Client A's portfolio is concentrated in a sector that just received a regulatory warning.** The agent shows: current exposure, downside risk modeling, 3 rebalancing options with tax implications for each, and how similar situations were handled for other clients. You review, pick option 2 with a modification, and approve. The Compliance Agent confirms it meets all regulatory requirements. Done.

2. **Client B just got divorced.** The agent detected this from a beneficiary change request. It's flagged this as a life event requiring human attention. It's prepared: current asset allocation, joint-to-individual transition plan, tax implications, beneficiary updates needed, and a suggested meeting agenda. You call Client B. You don't fumble for information. You listen.

3. **Client C has been moving money to a different institution.** The agent detected the pattern from account activity. It's not making assumptions about why, but it's surfaced the trend and suggested a check-in. This is a retention signal that no dashboard would have shown you, but an agent watching patterns caught immediately.

### Multi-client intelligence

Your Research Agent has identified that 28 of your clients are significantly underweight in a sector that its analysis suggests is undervalued. Instead of individually adjusting 28 portfolios:
- The agent groups them by risk profile and time horizon
- Proposes rebalancing strategies for each group
- Shows the aggregate impact
- You review the strategy, make one adjustment, and batch-approve

28 clients improved in 15 minutes of thoughtful review.

### Client meeting prep

Before any client meeting, the agent assembles:
- Portfolio performance vs. goals (visual, not spreadsheet)
- Life events since last meeting (job change, new baby, property purchase)
- Topics the client asked about last time and follow-up status
- Recommended discussion topics based on portfolio state and market conditions
- A one-page "what changed since we last spoke" brief for the client

You walk in prepared to have a real conversation, not to present data.

### Compliance

Every trade proposal, every client communication, every rebalancing decision is automatically logged with:
- The agent's reasoning
- The data it used
- The advisor's decision and any modifications
- Timestamp and audit trail

When auditors come, you don't scramble to reconstruct decisions. Everything is already documented, explainable, and complete.

## The Agent Team

**Portfolio Monitor Agent**
- Continuous surveillance of all client portfolios
- Detects drift from targets, risk exposure changes, concentration risks
- Correlates market events with client-specific impacts
- Outputs: alerts, rebalancing triggers, client-specific impact assessments

**Rebalancing Agent**
- Proposes specific trades when portfolios drift or opportunities arise
- Calculates tax implications for every proposed trade
- Groups similar client situations for efficient batch processing
- Outputs: trade proposals with reasoning, tax analysis, and batch groupings

**Compliance Agent**
- Reviews every proposed action against regulatory requirements
- Maintains audit trails automatically
- Flags anything that requires additional documentation or approval
- Outputs: compliance status per action, audit-ready documentation

**Client Intelligence Agent**
- Monitors client communications, account activity, and life events
- Detects sentiment shifts, concerns, and retention risks
- Tracks client goals and progress against them
- Outputs: client health scores, life event flags, meeting prep briefs

**Research Agent**
- Synthesizes market data, sector analysis, and economic indicators
- Identifies opportunities and risks relevant to specific client profiles
- Generates client-appropriate summaries (not analyst jargon)
- Outputs: market briefs, opportunity alerts, risk warnings

**Communication Agent**
- Drafts personalized client updates in the advisor's voice
- Handles routine client questions
- Delivers market context during volatility
- Outputs: drafted communications for advisor review, auto-sent routine updates

## The Experience in Detail

### The Command Center: 350 Clients at a Glance

Sarah opens her morning dashboard at 7:45 AM. She doesn't see a client list. She sees a client health view.

The screen is organized around a single question: who needs me today? At the top, three cards in amber and red. Below them, a compact batch-approval queue. Below that, a calm green field representing the 342 clients whose portfolios are tracking to plan.

The green field isn't just "everything's fine." Each client row carries a one-line outcome summary. Not process metrics. Outcomes:

- "Portfolio grew 8.2% this quarter, outperforming benchmark by 1.1%. On track for retirement target."
- "Down 1.4% this quarter, but within expected range for risk profile. 94% probability of meeting 5-year goal."
- "Education fund is 78% funded with 6 years remaining. Ahead of schedule."

There are no trade counts, no rebalancing logs, no execution reports. The system knows the advisor cares about whether clients are reaching their goals, not how many transactions it took to get there.

The three clients that need attention appear as proposal cards, each with a clear headline and a recommended action:

**Card 1: Client A, Sector Concentration Risk**
"Client A has 34% exposure to healthcare, which received an FDA regulatory warning yesterday. Downside risk model shows 8-12% potential loss in that sector over 90 days. Three rebalancing options prepared. Compliance: pre-cleared for all three."
Action button: "Review Options"

**Card 2: Client B, Life Event: Divorce Filing**
"Beneficiary change request detected Tuesday. Cross-referenced with public records. Asset split planning needed: joint accounts total $2.4M across 3 accounts. Meeting agenda drafted. Tax implications modeled for 4 scenarios."
Action button: "Open Client Workspace"

**Card 3: Client C, Retention Risk**
"Three outbound transfers totaling $180K over the past 6 weeks. Destination: Vanguard brokerage. No communication from client in 45 days. Last satisfaction score: 7/10 (down from 9/10). Suggested action: personal check-in call."
Action button: "Schedule Call"

Below the three cards, the batch-approval queue holds the five routine rebalancings. The header reads: "5 rebalancings within standard parameters. All compliance-cleared." Each one shows a one-line summary: "Client F: sell $12K large-cap growth, buy $12K international value. Drift correction: 3.2% back to target." At the bottom, two buttons: "Approve All" and "Review Individually." Sarah scans the summaries, sees nothing unusual, and hits "Approve All." Five clients improved before her first cup of coffee is done.

### The Approval Flow: Trade Proposals

Sarah taps "Review Options" on Client A's card. The proposal screen opens with three tabs, one for each rebalancing option. She starts with Option 2, the one the agent flagged as recommended.

The trade proposal is a single card with five sections:

**What:**
Sell 60% of HealthFirst Holdings (currently $142K of $418K position). Buy: split proceeds between Vanguard Total Market ETF ($50K), iShares Global Infrastructure ($45K), and increase existing Schwab US Dividend Equity position ($47K).

**Why:**
Healthcare sector now represents 34% of portfolio versus 22% target. FDA regulatory review creates concentrated downside risk. Proposed trades bring sector weight to 18%, slightly underweight to allow buffer. The three buy targets align with Client A's stated preference for dividend income and long-term growth. All three are already in the portfolio, so this deepens existing positions rather than adding complexity.

**Risk:**
Tax impact: estimated $18,400 in long-term capital gains, resulting in approximately $3,680 federal tax liability at Client A's bracket. This is the most tax-efficient of the three options because the HealthFirst position has the longest holding period. Market timing consideration: healthcare sector is down 6% from its 30-day high, so selling now locks in some loss versus peak, but the agent's model shows a 68% probability of further decline. Client sentiment note: Client A mentioned in March that she "likes healthcare as a long-term bet." This trade reduces but doesn't eliminate the position.

**Alternatives:**
Option 1: Sell only 40% of HealthFirst. Lower tax impact ($11,200 gain), but leaves portfolio at 24% healthcare, still above target and still concentrated. Risk reduction: moderate.
Option 3: Sell 80% of HealthFirst and diversify across 5 ETFs. Strongest risk reduction, but highest tax impact ($24,600 gain) and adds two new positions to the portfolio. More disruptive.

**Compliance:**
All three options pre-cleared. No wash-sale conflicts. No restricted securities involved. Suitability confirmed against Client A's risk questionnaire (last updated: 4 months ago). Note: risk questionnaire is due for annual refresh in 2 months.

Sarah likes Option 2 but wants to keep more of the HealthFirst position. She adjusts: "Keep 50% instead of 40%." She types the change into the adjustment field.

The agent recalculates in three seconds. The card updates in place: sell amount drops from $142K to $95K. Buy allocations rebalance proportionally. Tax impact drops to $12,100. Healthcare exposure lands at 23%, one point above target. The compliance status stays green. A new note appears: "At 23% healthcare, portfolio remains slightly overweight. Drift will likely trigger another rebalancing review in 60-90 days if sector doesn't self-correct."

Sarah reads the note, decides she's comfortable with a follow-up review, and hits "Approve." The trade enters execution. Time spent: four minutes.

### Conversation as Workspace: Client Meeting Prep

Sarah has a 2:00 PM meeting with Client B, the divorce case. At 1:15, she opens Client B's workspace. Instead of clicking through tabs and reports, she starts a conversation.

**Sarah:** "Brief me on Client B."

**Agent:** "Client B: Marcus Chen, age 52. Total managed assets: $2.4M across three accounts (joint brokerage: $1.6M, his IRA: $480K, his Roth IRA: $320K). Married 18 years to Diana Chen. Divorce filing detected on Tuesday via beneficiary change request.

Key financial picture: Joint brokerage holds the largest positions, including $340K in Apple stock purchased in 2014 with a cost basis of $62K. The IRA is allocated 70/30 equities to bonds, standard for his age and risk profile. The Roth IRA is fully invested in growth ETFs, which has been the strategy since 2019.

Immediate concerns: The joint brokerage account will need to be divided. The Apple position has significant embedded capital gains ($278K unrealized). How the account is split will have major tax consequences. Beneficiary designations on both retirement accounts currently name Diana.

I've modeled four asset-split scenarios and drafted a meeting agenda. His last meeting was March 14."

**Sarah:** "What did we discuss last time about the house?"

**Agent:** "In the March 14 meeting, Marcus mentioned they were considering selling the house in Lake Oswego, estimated value $1.2M with $340K remaining on the mortgage. He said Diana wanted to keep the house and he was open to that if the financial offset was fair. You noted that if Diana keeps the house, Marcus would need approximately $860K in liquid assets or equivalent value to balance the split. You also discussed whether the home equity line ($80K available, $0 drawn) should be closed before proceedings. You recommended he consult his attorney on that point. No follow-up was recorded."

**Sarah:** "What are the tax implications of splitting the Roth IRA?"

**Agent:** "Splitting a Roth IRA in divorce is generally tax-free if done through a transfer incident to divorce under a divorce decree or separation agreement. The key details for Marcus:

His Roth IRA ($320K) has been open since 2016, so it meets the 5-year rule for qualified distributions on his portion. If a portion is transferred to Diana via a court order, it becomes her Roth IRA. However, her 5-year clock may restart depending on whether she already has an existing Roth IRA.

The growth ETF positions inside the Roth don't create a tax event when transferred. No capital gains are triggered.

One consideration you flagged in your notes from January: Marcus made a $6,500 contribution in early January for this tax year. If the divorce finalizes before year-end and his modified AGI changes due to filing status, his contribution limit isn't affected (Roth limits apply individually, not jointly, and his income alone is $185K, well under the phase-out).

Recommendation: coordinate the Roth split timing with the overall settlement to avoid any complications with Diana's existing retirement accounts, if any. I don't have information on Diana's accounts since she isn't a client."

Sarah has her full context in three questions. She didn't open a single report, search through notes, or check a CRM. The agent connected information from meeting notes, account data, tax rules, and previous conversations into a coherent brief. She spends the remaining 30 minutes thinking about how to have a compassionate conversation with Marcus, not hunting for data.

### Trust Levels Across the Client Base

Not every client relationship works the same way, and the agent system reflects that. Sarah's 350 clients operate under different trust configurations based on three factors: client complexity, relationship length, and the agent's track record with that specific portfolio.

**High-net-worth, conservative client (Margaret, $8.2M):**
Margaret is 74, lives on portfolio income, and has told Sarah twice that she "doesn't want any surprises." Her trust configuration: the agent monitors and proposes, but every trade requires Sarah's explicit approval regardless of size. Even a $5K rebalancing within a bond ladder gets a proposal card. Margaret also receives a personal call from Sarah for any change, not just a notification. The agent knows this and adds "Client prefers phone discussion before execution" to every proposal.

**Standard portfolio with long history (David, $620K):**
David has been with Sarah for 9 years. His portfolio is a straightforward three-fund allocation. Over the past 3 years, the agent has executed 34 rebalancing trades for David's account, all within parameters, all with positive outcomes. David's trust configuration has evolved: the agent rebalances within a 5% drift band without approval and sends David a weekly summary. Trades outside the drift band or above $30K still require Sarah's sign-off. David gets a monthly email, auto-drafted by the Communication Agent in Sarah's voice, summarizing activity and performance.

**New client relationship (Priya, $410K, 3 months):**
Priya just transferred her accounts. Everything is supervised. The agent proposes, Sarah reviews every action, and Priya receives detailed explanations of each decision. The agent is learning Priya's patterns: her risk tolerance in practice (not just on the questionnaire), her communication preferences, her reaction to market volatility. In three months, the agent hasn't built enough history to earn expanded trust.

**How trust adjusts over time:**
The trust system isn't static. It tracks a confidence score based on the agent's track record with each specific client. For David's account, here's how trust evolved:

- Months 1-3: All rebalancing proposals required Sarah's approval. The agent executed 6 trades, all approved without modification.
- Months 4-6: Sarah moved David to "approve trades over $25K." The agent executed 11 smaller trades autonomously. All tracked to plan.
- Month 7: One trade underperformed expectations by 2.3%. The system flagged it but didn't downgrade trust because the underperformance was within normal variance.
- Month 9: Sarah reviewed the agent's 6-month track record for David (17 trades, 0 modifications needed, portfolio tracking 0.4% above benchmark) and expanded autonomy to "approve trades over $50K."
- Month 14 onward: Current configuration. The agent rebalances within drift bands freely. Sarah reviews quarterly and spot-checks occasionally.

The trust adjustment is never automatic. The system surfaces recommendations: "Agent has executed 34 trades for David Chen over 36 months with 0 modifications and +0.4% alpha. Current approval threshold: $50K. Suggested adjustment: $75K." Sarah decides. The human always holds the trust dial.

### Multi-Agent Choreography: A Rebalancing Event

When Sarah hit "Approve All" on those five routine rebalancings this morning, a chain of agent actions fired in sequence. Here's what happened behind the scenes for one of them: Client F's portfolio.

**Step 1: Portfolio Monitor Agent (triggered overnight at 2:14 AM)**
The Monitor detected that Client F's large-cap growth allocation had drifted to 28.2%, above the 25% target by more than the 3% threshold. It generated a rebalancing trigger and passed it to the Rebalancing Agent with the full portfolio snapshot.

**Step 2: Rebalancing Agent (2:14 AM, 4 seconds later)**
The Rebalancing Agent calculated the optimal trade: sell $12,400 of Vanguard Growth ETF (VUG), buy $12,400 of Vanguard International Value ETF (VTRIX). This brings large-cap growth to 24.8% and international value from 14.1% to 16.3%, closer to the 17% target. It checked Client F's tax lots and selected the lot with the longest holding period to minimize tax impact. It packaged the proposal and sent it to the Compliance Agent.

**Step 3: Compliance Agent (2:14 AM, 11 seconds later)**
The Compliance Agent ran its checks: suitability confirmation (Client F's risk score: 7/10, trade is consistent), wash-sale check (no purchase of substantially identical securities in the past 30 days: clear), concentration limits (no single position exceeds 10%: clear), regulatory holds (none). Status: approved. The proposal moved to Sarah's morning queue.

**Step 4: Sarah approves (7:48 AM)**
Sarah's "Approve All" triggered execution for all five trades.

**Step 5: Communication Agent (7:48 AM, 2 seconds after approval)**
The Communication Agent drafted Client F's notification: "Hi Frank, a small adjustment was made to your portfolio this morning to keep your investment mix aligned with your targets. We sold a small amount of US large-cap growth (which had grown above your target, a good problem to have) and added to your international holdings. No action needed from you. Your portfolio is on track." The message matched Sarah's casual, reassuring tone. It queued for delivery at 9:00 AM, during business hours.

**The flow is visible to Sarah as a timeline on each trade card:** Monitor → Rebalance → Compliance → Approved → Communication → Delivered. Five checkmarks in a row. She can expand any step to see the details, but for routine trades, she rarely does.

**When the choreography breaks:**

Two weeks ago, the same flow ran for Client M, but it didn't go smoothly.

The Portfolio Monitor flagged a rebalancing: sell $45K of an energy ETF, buy $45K of a clean energy fund. The Rebalancing Agent proposed the trade. The Compliance Agent rejected it.

The flow paused. Sarah's morning dashboard showed Client M's proposal card with a red compliance flag instead of the usual green checkmark. She tapped to see why.

"Compliance hold: Client M's investment policy statement specifies 'no fossil fuel divestment without client consultation.' The proposed sale of Energy Select SPDR (XLE) may conflict with this directive. Client M added this restriction in September after a discussion about aligning investments with energy sector employment."

The Rebalancing Agent, seeing the compliance rejection, had already generated an alternative: instead of selling the energy ETF, sell an overweight position in US mid-cap ($45K) and buy the clean energy fund. This achieves the diversification goal without touching the restricted holding. Compliance cleared the alternative.

Sarah saw both options side by side: the original (blocked, with the reason) and the alternative (cleared). She approved the alternative and made a note to discuss the energy allocation with Client M at their next quarterly review. The whole detour took her two minutes. Without the Compliance Agent catching the policy restriction, it could have become a client relationship problem and a regulatory finding.

## What This Teaches About Agent Design

1. **Trust architecture is the product.** The entire experience rests on graduated trust: the client trusts the advisor, the advisor trusts the agents, the regulator trusts the audit trail. If any link breaks, the whole thing fails.

2. **The best use of agents isn't speed, it's depth.** The advisor doesn't just serve more clients. They serve each client more deeply because they arrive at every interaction fully prepared and fully present.

3. **Proactive > reactive is the fundamental shift.** Today's financial advisory is reactive (client calls, advisor responds). The agent-managed version is proactive at every level: the agent watches markets, the agent detects life events, the agent surfaces opportunities.

4. **Agent personality must match the domain.** A financial agent must communicate confidence levels honestly. "I'm confident" vs. "There's meaningful uncertainty" isn't just nice to have: in finance, it's a regulatory and ethical requirement.

5. **The invisible work is where agents shine most.** The client never sees the Compliance Agent. But it's the reason the advisor can approve 28 trades in 15 minutes without worrying about regulatory risk.
